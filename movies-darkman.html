Darkman (1990)

Superhero movies have become incredibly popular and mainstream. It isn't unreasonable to assume that the person reading this has seen at least a couple films of that genre. However, this wasn't the case in the nineties. Back then, superhero movies were few and far between, while arguably producing more interesting projects.

Darkman is an example of a darker superhero flick, one that in all likelihood wouldn't be too popular nowadays. The plot centers around Peyton Westlake, a doctor that worked on developing a special tissue capable of replicating human skin, before gangsters break down his lab, kill his co-worker, and seemingly kill Westlake himself. However, he survives and uses the tissue technology to impersonate various mobsters, destroying the organization from inside.

The movie was one of the films my parents had on VHS tapes, so I have very early memories of seeing it as a kid. Back then, I didn't understand a whole lot, but the memory of the film's vibe stuck with me. When I was a teenager, I got familiarized with the works of Sam Raimi, which led to my re-discovery of Darkman.

Sam Raimi's imaginitive and unique abilities as a director are, in my opinion, at their peak in Darkman. The film takes the best elements of the Evil Dead franchise and combines it with techniques Raimi would soon use in his Spider-Man trilogy. All of this makes Darkman an enjoyable and memorable, if not a bit chaotic watch, getting a strong recommednation from me to all the fans of dark superheroes and film noire.
